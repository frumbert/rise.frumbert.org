Key risk factors for Pressure Injuries

Physicians should be familiar with patient-level and system-level risk factors that increase the likelihood of pressure injury development. (Hover over a word or phrase to review its definition.)

  1. Immobility(opens in a new tab)(opens in a new tab)Patients who are bedbound, sedated, paralyzed, or post-surgical are at significantly increased risk due to prolonged pressure on bony prominences. Critically ill patients exhibit high rates of PIs due to multiple overlapping risk factors, including immobility, oxygenation deficits, and poor perfusion. [25]

  2. Nutritional deficits(opens in a new tab)(opens in a new tab)Malnutrition, dehydration, and hypoalbuminemia impair tissue repair and resilience. [16]

  3. Incontinence(opens in a new tab)(opens in a new tab)Moisture from urine or stool macerates the skin and accelerates breakdown. [21]

  4. Perfusion deficits(opens in a new tab)(opens in a new tab)Hypotension, vasopressor use, and vascular disease impair tissue oxygenation and circulation. [25]

  5. things Comorbidities(opens in a new tab)Conditions like diabetes, advanced age, and spinal cord injury increase risk due to neuropathy, limited mobility, and altered tissue response. [7]

  6. Surgery: Prolonged operative time(opens in a new tab)(particularly >3 hours), hypothermia, and poor preoperative condition elevate PI risk in the surgical setting. Cumulative procedures and use of nerve blocks may also contribute by masking pain and decreasing protective movement. [15,24]

  7. Spinal cord injury (SCI):(opens in a new tab)Lack of sensation and inability to reposition independently are central contributors. Additional risk factors include bowel management systems, hypotension, and cervical collar use. Ischial and sacral injuries are especially common during the rehabilitation phase. [25]

  8. Prone positioning: Common in spine and COVID-related ventilation protocols, this orientation increases risk to facial structures and anterior chest due to limited adipose tissue and padding, with additional threat from tightly secured devices. [20]

  9. Obesity: Although once assumed to offer protection, obesity presents unique challenges. Skin folds produce tissue-on-tissue pressure and trap moisture, increasing risk for breakdown and infection. Maceration and reduced ability to inspect or offload skin further exacerbate vulnerability. [25]

  10. Scar tissue: Scarred areas are less resilient, lacking sweat glands, nerve endings, and elasticity, which makes them prone to shearing forces. These areas do not trigger protective repositioning due to impaired sensory feedback. [25]

  11. Device-related injuries: Medical devices (nasal cannulas, collars, tubes) can exert focal pressure and are often overlooked in repositioning routines. Devices applied before fluid shifts (e.g., edema from resuscitation) may become overly tight and cause injuries including Medical Adhesive Related Skin Injury (MARSI). [3,20]

  12. Dyspnea and head injuries: These patients often assume fixed positions (e.g., tripod, elevated HOB), which reduce movement and increase localized pressure, particularly on the sacrum and ischial tuberosities. [25]

  13. Heels: The heel is the second most common site for PIs, due to thin soft tissue coverage and proximity to bone. Underlying diseases such as atherosclerosis, neuropathy, and arthritis elevate risk. Friction from linens also contributes, especially in confused or restless patients. [25]

  14. Prior or existing pressure injury: A history of PIs significantly increases the risk of recurrence, particularly if scarring or reduced mobility persists. [9]
